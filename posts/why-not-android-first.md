## I Tried Android First. It Didn't Work.

When I decided to build Little Wins as a mobile app, Android was the obvious choice. I had the APK ready. I found 12 users for testing. I was going to ship.

That was 15 days ago.

## What Happened

I'm introverted. I have ADHD. Testing with 12 real users meant:

- Coordinating installs
- Answering questions
- Handling crashes
- Managing expectations
- Following up

For a neurotypical person, this is normal. For me, it was paralyzing. Every notification from a tester sent my anxiety up. Every bug report felt like a failure. I stopped opening the test group chat.

The path was blocked — not by code, but by the social overhead of Android testing.

## The Pivot

A few days ago I realized: iOS doesn't need this.

With the iPhone App Store:
- No APK sideloading
- No coordinating test installs
- No explaining "enable unknown sources"
- TestFlight handles distribution automatically
- Users install like any other app

It removes the entire social friction layer that was blocking me.

## What I Learned

1. **Know your brain.** Android's open model is great for power users but terrible for an introvert with ADHD who just wants to ship without talking to people.

2. **The platform matters.** The rules and workflows of each platform affect not just your users, but your ability to deliver. iOS's walled garden works better for me.

3. **15 days stuck is 15 days too long.** If a path is blocked, switch paths. Don't keep banging your head against a wall because you committed to the wrong approach.

4. **Build in public means sharing the failures too.** This post is embarrassing to write. That's exactly why I'm writing it.

## What's Next

The iPhone app is ready. The Mac app is already running. I'm submitting to the App Store this week — no test group coordination needed.
